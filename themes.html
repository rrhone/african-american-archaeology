Themes

These themes highlight how African American archaeology approaches power, representation, community collaboration, and everyday life.

Power

African American archaeology questions whose voices appear in official archives. By comparing artifacts with written records, archaeologists show how power shaped what was recorded and what was ignored.

  • Key idea: Documents often reflect the perspectives of slaveholders, employers, or officials rather than Black communities.
  • Archaeological contribution: Material evidence helps recover labor, family, and resistance that are missing or distorted in the written sources.
  • Example sources: Scholarship by Maria Franklin and Charles Orser on plantations, households, and race.

Representation

Many projects involve descendant communities who have direct ties to the sites being studied. Oral histories, family memory, and community priorities help guide research questions and interpretation.

  • Key idea: Archaeology is not only about the past, but also about how people today want their histories told.
  • Archaeological contribution: Collaborative work can reshape museum displays, signage, and public narratives about Black history.
  • Example sources: Case studies that involve churches, neighborhood groups, and family networks.

Accountability

African American archaeology treats plantations, urban blocks, military posts, and cemeteries as connected landscapes. These spaces show how Black people created home, community, and politics across time.

  • Key idea: Sites of slavery and freedom are linked through migration, labor, and memory.
  • Archaeological contribution: Mapping houses, artifact clusters, and paths through space reveals how people moved, gathered, and organized daily life.
  • Example sources: Research that traces households from plantations into post-emancipation towns and cities.
